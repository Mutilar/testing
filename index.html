Our Recent Interview

Alexis: Good afternoon I am Alexis Interiano and I am here with Alejandra Munoz. Today on fishy new we are here to talk about the Cosumnes River with Brian Hungerman a student at the University of California, Merced who is working with Professor Joshua Viers in the department of environmental science. Much of the research done here in on the Cosumnes river one of the last undammed rivers in California and there has been an awful lot of talk about wanting to dam the river.

Ale: Exactly, as a society dammed rivers are normal, but with these proposals we are hearing about how damming the river could negatively impact the ecosystem and communities that are involved with the river. Many of our readers has requested that we get down to the truth behind these arguments, because they also see something fishy going on.

Alexis: The Miwok tribe they resided in the area many years ago, but how did their actions in respecting the ecosystem in the past affect the current condition of the river?

Brian: You know that's a great question Alex, I think understanding first of all the history of the river will help us understand where the conversation is going now. The Miwok tribe resided in the area for hundreds of years before European civilizations came into the region. Up until the gold rush really is when they were forced out of their habitat they lived around the Cosumnes River, living of off the native salmon, and local vegetation and they were able to have a symbiotic relationship with the river. Where they never took more than they needed and they always give back to the ecosystem to ensure their presence there did not hamper the ecosystem from thriving. It’s important to realize that the tribe could only thrive in this region because the river was undammed. The undammed nature of the river allowed for seasonal flooding which which promoted the growth of local vegetation which the Miwok people live off of as well as support of the presence of the salmon in the river.

Ale: As we all know the endangered Valley Oak Tree is actually native to the river what about the undamnedness of the river actually allows it to thrive there?

Brian: That’s a great question I think it’s important to know the context of the cosumnes River in this sense. The Cosumnes River is the only river in the Central Valley that is not damned. Now damming is an important part of the ecosystem because when a river is undamned it floods every season this flooding is nature's way of flushing out brush that is overpowering other plants and preventing their growth. You can imagine it like a rose bush you have to trim some leaves off of the rosebush to allow it to flower again this active flooding allows more vegetation to grow and the area.

Alexis: So Brian you keep coming back to the flora and you mentioned the Miwok depending on the resources and an undammed river. Now how would salmons success or viability thrive in these conditions.

Brian: Well it’s important to know that salmon was the tribes primary source of protein for hundreds of years. So the lifecycle of the salmon is important to understand. They breed and spawn upstream in the Cosumnes River. They swim all the way out to the Pacific ocean for the majority of their lives after they have matured they swim back up the river to their original spawning grounds and the cycle repeats. Now you throw a dam into this equation these salmon cannot get back to the original spawning grounds and die without having reproduced. For the Miwok people the salmon not returning was a life or death situation as is exactly what happen in the 1840s. As the first settlers from the goldrush constructed small levees and dams along the Cosumnes River for agricultural purposes.

Ale: You continuously go back to the Miwok people surviving in the area off of the salmon and we do know that they were driven out of their homes but where are they now?

Brian: I mean it’s a sad story. The tribe was very successful up until when the gold rush happened but in the 1840s many settlers from across the world came to the Central Valley with the hopes of striking it rich by finding gold. Most of this gold was originally found in rivers and streams so early settlers flocked to the river with the hopes of finding gold in it. You know after a few years they found there was not much gold to be found in the Cosumnes River so it was largely ignored. However, as civilization began to flourish in the Central Valley the need for agriculture and farming increased so there was a market then to alter some of the watersheds in the region to support the large agricultural project such as the growing of wheat and grain. There were some unintended side effects that the original settlers could not have foreseen one of the first things that affected was the salmon and not being able to reproduce and very quickly saw them not appearing in the river much longer after the 1840s. Now the Miwok people depending on the salmon for the majority of their protein, and without the salmon returning the Miwok people did not know what was happening to the salmon they were just continuing to fish and not finding fish in their waters so in desperation they moved out of the region for search of more food. They never really established anywhere else after that partially also due to the presence of more European colonies in the area it’s sad to say but in the late 1990s the last person that spoke the planes Miwok language passed away. Unfortunately there are people who have descended from the Miwok people yes that are still around but much of their language and culture has been lost to history. That something that we all hate to see and we are going to want to do much as we can and to not let these sorts of tragedies happen in the future.

Alexis: You talk about these temporary dam projects how would damming the river be the cause for these effects such as the salmon depletion.

Brian: YeahI mean it’s a complex biological system again the main issue here is when the salmon are trying to return upstream into their normal breeding grounds. When you throw a big line in the sand and they can’t cross over into where they want to go where they want to breed the salmon pass away.

Alexis: So you are talking about temporary damming projects during the gold rush. But we are talking about how the Cosumnes River is undamed what happen there?

Brian: I mean of course the Cosumnes River has a long history during the gold rush some levees and dams were constructed along the river to control it for more agricultural uses. In the 1970s I want to say there were some environmental moments that started to the creation of the Cosumnes River Preserve when they started realizing that these modifications to the river were negatively affecting many animals and that ecosystem these actions were taken to preserve the watershed for future generations. In the formation of the Cosumnes River Preserve many of these original Gold Rush era modifications were removed to try and get it back to that natural free-flowing state that allowed for such things as natural flooding which is crucial for the flora in that region.

Ale: You mention that the were projects that were started in the past, but what project are there now and what research have you actually been doing with Professor Veirs?

Brian: The Cosumnes River is a point of focus for the vice lab we are currently doing research to understand how these sorts of modifications to rivers can impact the ecosystems and watersheds that they are in. So some current research is going on that quite fascinates to me personally is actually trying to engineer artificial flooding along the Cosumnes River. So we are actually breaking apart the sides of the river banks along to Cosumnes River so the river floods more land in the rainy season and we are monitoring the vegetation growth in these regions and these artificially flooded zones to understand how important flooding is for the reinstatement of endangered flora.

Alexis: Let me get this straight we are not damming the river, but you mentioned how working with the vice labs bulldozers are coming in and working on the river and helping it out, so there is some type of human contact with the river and projects going on.

Brian: Right so I might seem kind of contradictory that we are still manipulating the flow of the river but it is important to understand that we are not trying to hold back the river anymore we’re actually trying to get the river back to its original state. That of the terrain around it so that it is as free flowing as possible and floods the most area as possible, because for every acre we are increasing in the rivers floodplain we are increasing the amount of biomass and flora and fauna in that region exponentially. So there are a lot of efforts right now going on to try and make small edits to the river to allow to reclaim the watershed as a did before the gold rush.

Ale: So you talk a lot about how the people you are working with are specifically trying to positively impact the river but there are some proposals that are coming up about people who want to dam the river how do you feel about that and what’s your opinion

Brian: You know it’s hard for me given my experience with the Cosumnes I’ve been working very closely with the Cosumnes for over a year now. I kind of have the inside scoop as to why the Cosumnes is such a magical place not only for researchers like me but also for the general public you know. I guess these proposals do makes sense from a civil engineering perspective flooding is damaging and has damaged in the past farms and cities in the region. Most notably in the early 1900s Sacramento was actually damage from flooding from the same watershed that the Cosumnes River is in so it's understandable to see that these city architects are concerned letting such a free flowing river be so close to these large civilizations. Of course it is important to mention that basically all of the central valley is heavily urbanized and is hard to find any place that we can truly allow nature to be natural. Yes this flooding is actually still a double edge sword yes it can cause much damage to local infrastructure but it is also vitally important for the ecosystem. You know we’re talking earlier about the the Valley Oak tree this tree is actually the main reason why there haven't been dams constructed on it so far and it is actually the reason that so many proposals to dam the river have been for the most part denied up until this point. There has been quite a bit of research suggesting that if we had damned the Cosumnes river there’s a high likelihood that the Valley Oak Tree could go extinct you know so from my experience it would be quite flippant to think that the prevention of a few million dollars of property damage would justify completely removing one species from this planet and decimating the flora of this watershed. So in this sense damming the river would actually be much more destructive than letting it floods seasonally.

Alexis: Staying on the thought of preventing the damming of the river what others ways would keeping the river undammed benefit society

Brian: Well Alex there are numerous primarily this is a lot of what we have been talking about today and of course you could have podcasts full of information of other ways why the Cosumnes needs to stay undamned in my expert opinion. So it functions importantly as a wildlife preserve the Cosumnes Wildlife Preserve features many endangered plants and animals in the region, but in addition to this there are also numerous things that would be affected if we dammed the river. In my field this is an important research opportunity studying the Consonus River and how it reacts to man-made manipulation. The research that we are doing on the Cosumnes river is not only applicable to the Central Valley but to all of California and the world. When other nations are considering environmental projects like dams they can look to the Cosumnes River as an important case study for seeing why it is important to keep nature natural. Lastly there is the idea of ecotourism the Cosumnes River has been a popular destination for locals for such activities as kayaking, camping, and really enjoying and immersing themselves in nature and we don’t want to ruin that opportunity for future generations

Ale: Well a lot of the information you gave us today is very interesting what if some of your audience has suddenly become very interested in helping with this project how can they do that.

Brian: Well that’s a great question I think if we want to keep pushing the sorts of research opportunities forward first of all we need to keep the Cosumnes River undammed. Ways you can support that as you are saying is by taking a day trip out to the Cosumnes Preserve there’s a great Visitor Center there. Even if you are immersed in nature you don’t realize that you are so close to Sacramento and to all these big destinations because you kind of get lost in the foliage. Also let your local representative know that you are unhappy with there sorts of proposals to dam the Cosumnes River, because right now the people who are writing these proposals are in the end trying to help the local communities and prevent damage from flooding. Let’s make sure that our representatives know that we also appreciate having nature around us and not trying to control nature too much. If we can get our representatives in the California Senate to be aware of these sorts of environmental issues I think it would help a lot into making sure that these proposals don’t get much further than they are now. Lastly I think that the easiest thing for us to do it that isn’t very time-consuming is to let your peers know about this reach out to them on social media let people know what’s going on with the Cosumnes and why this is an issue that affects all of us.

Alexis: Well unfortunately that’s all we have time for, but Brian if they like to get contact with you what’s the best way to contact you.

Brian: Of course Alex and Alejandra I appreciate you guys having me on Fishy News if you wanna learn more or you can go to consumingthecosumnes.com. That’s where you can reach out to me and learn more about what our research lab is doing. Thanks guys.

Alex: Well again thank you very much for coming out and talking to us Brian. Thank you to our audience for joining us today on our episode of Fishy news. We hope that you'll join us next week for an exclusive on the fishy effects of air pollution.

Editorial

The Cosumnes River is the last remaining canary in the coal mine against the Central Valley’s unchecked and unregulated agroindustrial complex

Overview

The following sources build towards a general consensus: riparian wetlands in California’s Central Valley continue to be threatened; the Cosumnes River offers a unique research opportunity to understand naturally-flooding wetlands; and the agro-industrial complex continues to be the greatest threat to the Central Valley’s biodiversity, conservation movements, and water conservation.

Broken into five distinct sections, the following bibliography outlines the resources utilized to put together the puzzle pieces in the story of the Cosumnes River and how it acts as a canary in the coal mine for the Central Valley’s unchecked and unregulated agroindustrial complex.
